Ten Principles of Economics
Decision Making Interaction The Whole Economy

An Interactive Guide

by Jagriti Dodeja and Hardik Tiwari

Economics is the study of how society manages its scarce resources. These ten principles are the foundation of economic thinking. Explore them through interactive examples to see how they apply in the real world.

Part 1: How People Make Decisions

These first four principles cover the fundamentals of individual choice and decision making.

1. People Face Trade-offs

To get one thing, we usually have to give up something else. This includes the major societal trade-off between efficiency (size of the economic pie) and equality (how the pie is divided).

When government policies are designed, the goals of efficiency and equality often conflict. Policies aimed at equalizing distribution, like welfare, can reduce the reward for working hard, leading to a smaller economic pie. This visualization shows how focusing on one goal can impact the other.

More Equality More Efficiency

2. The Cost of Something Is What You Give Up to Get It

The true cost of an item is its opportunity cost: what you give up to get it. This often includes more than just money.

The largest opportunity cost of attending college is often the wages given up by not working. Use this calculator to see the true cost of a year of college.

3. Rational People Think at the Margin

Rational people make decisions by comparing the marginal costs and marginal benefits of small, incremental changes to a plan.

An airline is deciding whether to sell a last-minute ticket. The average cost per seat is high, but the marginal cost is low. What should it do?

A 200-seat plane costs $100,000 to fly.

Average Cost per Seat: $500

A standby passenger offers $300 for an empty seat.

Marginal Cost (cost of one more passenger): ~ $5 (for a soda)

YES! Sell the ticket.

The marginal benefit ($300) is greater than the marginal cost (~$5).

4. People Respond to Incentives

An incentive is something that induces a person to act. Changes in costs or benefits lead to predictable changes in behavior.

Seat belt laws provide a fascinating example of incentives. While they make drivers safer in an accident (the direct effect), they also reduce the "cost" of driving recklessly (the indirect effect). See how this alters behavior.

Part 2: How People Interact

The next three principles concern how people interact with one another in the economy.

5. Trade Can Make Everyone Better Off

Trade allows countries to specialize in what they do best and to enjoy a greater variety of goods and services.

Instead of being self-sufficient, individuals and countries gain by trading with others. This allows for specialization, which increases total output.

Before Trade (Isolation)

Country A: 10 Food, 5 Clothing

Country B: 5 Food, 10 Clothing

Total: 15 Food, 15 Clothing

After Trade (with Specialization)

Country A (makes food): 25 Food, 0 Clothing

Country B (makes clothes): 0 Food, 25 Clothing

Total: 25 Food, 25 Clothing (Gains!)

6. Markets Are Usually a Good Way to Organize Economic Activity

Market economies, guided by an "invisible hand" of prices and self-interest, have proven remarkably successful at organizing economic activity.

Adam Smith's "invisible hand" describes how prices guide the decisions of individual households and firms to outcomes that, in many cases, maximize society's well-being without central planning.

Prices are the instrument with which the invisible hand directs economic activity.

7. Governments Can Sometimes Improve Market Outcomes

Governments can improve market outcomes by enforcing property rights and correcting market failures.

The "invisible hand" is powerful, but not omnipotent. Government intervention is sometimes needed to promote efficiency or equality. A market failure occurs when the market on its own fails to produce an efficient allocation of resources.

Two main causes of market failure:

  • Externality: The impact of one person's actions on a bystander (e.g., pollution).
  • Market Power: The ability of a single actor to unduly influence market prices (e.g., a monopoly).

Part 3: How the Economy as a Whole Works

The final three principles concern the workings of the entire economy.

8. A Country's Standard of Living Depends on Its Ability to Produce Goods and Services

Almost all variation in living standards is attributable to differences in countries' productivity.

Productivity is the amount of goods and services produced from each unit of labor input. Higher productivity leads to a higher standard of living.

9. Prices Rise When the Government Prints Too Much Money

When a government creates large quantities of the nation's money, the value of the money falls, causing inflation.

In almost all cases of large or persistent inflation, the culprit is growth in the quantity of money. Click the button to see the effect.

10. Society Faces a Short-Run Trade-off between Inflation and Unemployment

In the short run, many economic policies push inflation and unemployment in opposite directions.

This trade-off is key to understanding the business cycle. Policymakers can influence the economy's position on this curve, but they face a difficult choice.
